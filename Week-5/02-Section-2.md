What Makes a Good User Story? (The INVEST Framework)

The INVEST framework, originally proposed by Bill Wake, serves as a checklist to ensure that product backlog items are high quality and ready for development. When a story meets these criteria, it reduces friction during the sprint and increases the likelihood of delivering a working product.


Why These Criteria Matter
``` bash
Criterion                               Why it's critical
INDEPENDENT                             Dependencies lead to "bottlenecks." If Story A depends on Story B, and    Story B is delayed, the whole sprint might fail.


NEGOTIABLE                              A story is not a fixed contract; it is a placeholder for a conversation. The "how" is decided through dialogue between the Product Owner and the Developers.

VALUABLE                                Every story must provide a clear benefit to the end user or the business. If it doesn't, it’s likely just technical "noise" or a sub-task.

ESTIMABLE                               If a team can't estimate a story, it usually means the story is too vague or the team lacks the necessary domain knowledge.

SMALL                                   Large stories (Epics) are hard to track and carry high risk. Small stories help maintain a steady flow of work (velocity).

TESTABLE                                If you can't write a test for it, you can't define "Done." This usually requires clear Acceptance Criteria.
```

In summary;
- Independent – Can be developed separately
- Negotiable – Details can be discussed
- Valuable – Delivers value to a user
- Estimable – Can be sized
- Small – Can fit within a sprint
- Testable – Has clear acceptance criteria

If a story fails multiple INVEST checks, it needs refinement.
